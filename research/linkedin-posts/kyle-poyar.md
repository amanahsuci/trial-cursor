## Post 1 — April 2026

CMOs are learning how to use a terminal just to adopt Claude Code.

I didn't expect that when I set out to survey 200 GTM operators about how they're using Claude Code and Cowork (alongside my GTM bestie Maja Voje of Go-To-Market Strategist). But here we are.

The short version: GTM folks without formal coding skills are now among the most enthusiastic Claude Code users. And they're not just saving time. 67% said these products enabled something previously (genuinely) impossible.

What stood out to me from the data:

1. GTM operators split almost evenly between Chat (30%), Code (31%), and Cowork (32%) as their primary Claude product.
2. The average user has adopted 3.5 different use cases — content creation and GTM engines are the two biggest.
3. 55% have already replaced a tool or vendor — usually ChatGPT, an agency, or a contractor.
4. The most common GTM workflows: social posts, building landing pages, SDR tooling, and signal-based outbound sequences.
5. The biggest complaint: credits. Half of respondents mentioned it unprompted. Even Max plan users are getting throttled 2-3x/day.
6. Claude replaces some tools while making others more valuable. What Claude power users love: ChatGPT (surprising), Clay & HubSpot.

## Post 2 — April 2026

SaaS stocks are down 20% this year according to Redpoint. It's the worst performing sector in the S&P 500. The median public SaaS company now trades at a 4.1x forward multiple — the lowest of the past decade.

WTF does this mean for "legacy" SaaS right now?

CRM (the OG of SaaS) is the perfect case study for why SaaS suddenly feels a lot like print newspapers in the early 00s. When I started my solopreneur business last year, I waited 6 months to adopt a CRM. I've used the big ones before and the idea of setting up another was… not exactly appealing. I figured I could just use a spreadsheet (although that was clearly breaking as sales ramped up). I then found Lightfield, an AI-native CRM that assembles itself.

An AI-native CRM isn't a regular CRM with a prompt bar. It's a system of intelligence + execution that works with you. Here's the difference:

1. **Setup** — Old CRM: Takes weeks or months, highly manual, starts empty. AI-native CRM: Connect email & calendar → it builds itself.
2. **Adoption** — Old CRM: Reps hate updating it (so they don't). AI-native CRM: Automatically captures emails, calls, meetings; CRM becomes a byproduct of doing work.
3. **Data model** — Old CRM: Acts as a database with structured fields (stages, checkboxes, etc.). AI-native CRM: Stores raw conversations & full context, builds insights on top — full details don't get over-simplified into a checkbox.
4. **Insights** — Old CRM: Dashboard-first; dashboards are only as good as the data. AI-native CRM: Get answers via prompt; save workflows as skills; can give you answers & then tell you what to do next.

Once you see an AI-native CRM that does work for you, you can't unsee it.

## Post 3 — April 2026

Salesforce going 'headless' is just the latest in a bigger trend: the SaaS empire (finally) strikes back.

On Thursday Marc Benioff announced that all of Salesforce & Slack are now exposed to AI agents, meaning you don't need to buy a seat to access Salesforce. They're 'ripping off the band-aid' — betting the long-term value from optimizing for AI agents is >>> near-term losses in seat licenses.

SaaS stocks are down 20% in 2026. Public SaaS companies now trade at a measly 4.1x revenue — the lowest multiple of the past decade. SaaS is getting compared to print newspapers (ouch!). I was even at a SaaS event last week (SaaStock) that re-branded during the conference (to Shift AI).

But I'm not ready to count SaaS out just yet.

SaaS is still quite sticky. And SaaS companies have advantages they can choose to exploit in this AI era:

1. It's extremely onerous to build and maintain homegrown enterprise software, even if Claude Code makes it technically feasible.
2. Systems of record like Salesforce or ServiceNow sit on extremely valuable data that AI-native companies need.
3. Many enterprise buyers would prefer to adopt new AI capabilities from their existing vendors rather than vet net-new startups.
4. SaaS companies have lots of $$$ and built-in distribution to invest heavily & win the AI market. Salesforce is going 'headless'. HubSpot slashed the price of their Breeze Customer Agent by 50%. Rippling says their AI launch has accelerated revenue growth to 78% y/y (at ARR over $1B).

There's a bull case for SaaS, although it won't be easy to pull off.

## Key Themes

- Non-technical GTM operators are among the fastest Claude Code adopters — the barrier to AI tooling is falling faster than expected
- AI-native vs. legacy SaaS is not just a UI difference — it's a fundamentally different data model, adoption pattern, and insight layer
- SaaS valuation compression is real (4.1x forward multiple, decade low), but incumbents have structural moats: data, distribution, and enterprise trust
- Credits and throttling are the top friction point for Claude power users — a gap between product promise and daily usability
- Seat-based pricing is under pressure — Salesforce going headless signals the industry is repricing around AI agent access, not human seats
