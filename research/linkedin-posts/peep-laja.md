## Post 1 — April 2026

"Contact sales" is a confession.

I asked 50 VPs of Product, CMOs and CEOs at B2B SaaS companies one question: what does your pricing page do?

- 27 of them hide pricing entirely.
- 14 show SMB tiers but gate enterprise.
- Only 9 are fully transparent.

The reasons for hiding are the same three, over and over:

- "Competitors would use it against us."
- "Our pricing is too complex to publish."
- "We discount so much, list price is meaningless."

Every one of these is a tell.

If competitors can weaponize your pricing, your positioning isn't strong enough to justify it.

If your pricing is "too complex to publish," your buyer thinks it's complex too. That's how deals die in procurement.

If you discount so heavily that list price is fiction, you're training every AE to cave and every buyer to push harder.

Meanwhile, the transparent aren't naive. They've made a bet: buyers self-qualify faster when they can see prices, and bad-fit deals die before sales wastes time.

"Contact sales" used to mean premium. Now it often means "we don't know what to charge you." Show your price. Or explain why you can't.

## Post 2 — April 2026

Most B2B SaaS is guessing at price.

I asked 50 VPs and CEOs how they test pricing before rolling it out. Only 18% do anything rigorous. A/B tests, conjoint analysis, willingness-to-pay studies — the stuff pricing consultants charge $200k for.

Here's the actual breakdown:

- 34% "tested" by asking a few friendly customers what they thought
- 28% never tested at all
- 20% ran live sales experiments — rep A pitches price X, rep B pitches price Y
- 10% run A/B tests on the pricing page
- 8% do formal conjoint or WTP research

That's it. That's the whole sample.

One VP told me: "I've always been skeptical about how one would even do that, given that asking customers what they want to pay doesn't work."

She's right that asking "what would you pay?" is useless. But conjoint, anchored cohort tests, Van Westendorp — these exist. They work. And they're within reach of any company over $10M ARR.

The same people "guessing" at price are the same people who told me in another question they wish they had better data on price elasticity and WTP.

You can wish for the data. Or you can go collect it.

Most pricing "decisions" in B2B SaaS aren't decisions. They're vibes.

Test before you ship.

## Post 3 — April 2026

Nobody owns pricing in B2B SaaS.

I asked 50 VPs and CEOs a simple question: who owns pricing decisions at your company?

- 38% said "cross-functional committee."
- 20% said "Other" — write-ins included things like "a mix of product marketing, CRO and CFO," "Chief Product Officer plus CFO," or "General Managers of our portfolio brands."

That's not ownership. That's a group chat.

The rest split five ways: CEO 16%, VP Product 14%, CFO 12%. One company had a Chief Pricing Officer. One had the CMO run it through a Market Intelligence team.

58% of B2B SaaS companies have no single human accountable for pricing.

Which is why so much of the data in this survey reads like chaos:

- 54% have never formally tested price
- Most pricing pages are gated because "it's complicated"
- The #1 mistake people named was "we underpriced and waited too long to fix it"

When pricing is everyone's job, it's nobody's job.

Name the owner.

## Key Themes

- Pricing transparency as a strategic signal — hiding pricing often reveals weak positioning, not smart strategy
- Most B2B SaaS pricing is driven by intuition, not data — rigorous methods (conjoint, Van Westendorp, WTP studies) exist but are rarely used
- Diffuse pricing ownership is a root cause of pricing dysfunction — committees and "group chats" produce no accountability
- Chronic underpricing is the #1 self-reported mistake — and most companies wait too long to fix it
- The gap between wanting better pricing data and actually collecting it is a choice, not a constraint
